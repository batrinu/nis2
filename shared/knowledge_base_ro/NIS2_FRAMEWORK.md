# NIS2 Framework Knowledge Base

## Directive (EU) 2022/2555 Summaries

### Overview

The NIS2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555) on measures for a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union entered into force on 16 January 2023. Member States must transpose it into national law by 17 October 2024.

### Key Objectives

1. **Achieve high common level of cybersecurity** across EU Member States
2. **Expand scope** to cover more sectors and entity types
3. **Strengthen security requirements** for essential and important entities
4. **Improve incident reporting** and response coordination
5. **Enhance supply chain security** throughout the digital ecosystem
6. **Increase enforcement powers** of competent authorities

---

## Article Summaries

### Chapter I - General Provisions

#### Article 1 - Subject Matter
Establishes measures to achieve high common level of cybersecurity within the Union through:
- National cybersecurity frameworks
- Cross-border cooperation mechanisms
- Risk management and reporting obligations

#### Article 2 - Scope
**In scope:**
- Essential Entities (EE) as defined in Annex I
- Important Entities (IE) as defined in Annex II
- Medium and large enterprises in identified sectors
- Certain entities regardless of size (DNS, TLD, trust services, public admin)

**Out of scope:**
- Small and micro enterprises (with sector exceptions)
- National security entities (when acting in that capacity)
- Public sector bodies carrying out activities in areas of national security

#### Article 3 - Definitions
Key definitions:
- **Cybersecurity:** Activities to protect network and information systems
- **Network and Information System:** Electronic communications network or device
- **Essential Entity:** Entity listed in Annex I meeting criteria in Article 24
- **Important Entity:** Entity listed in Annex II meeting criteria in Article 25
- **Significant Incident:** Incident defined in Article 23(3)
- **Competent Authority:** Authority designated under Article 8

### Chapter II - National Frameworks

#### Article 4 - National Cybersecurity Strategy
Member States must adopt national strategies covering:
- Objectives and priorities
- Governance and regulatory framework
- Risk assessment approach
- Awareness raising and training
- Research and development
- International cooperation

#### Article 5 - National Cybersecurity Crisis Management Framework
Requirements for crisis management including:
- Designation of CSIRTs
- Crisis management structures
- Cross-sector coordination

#### Articles 6-7 - CSIRTs and Competent Authorities
Designation and responsibilities of:
- Computer Security Incident Response Teams
- Competent authorities for supervision
- Single points of contact

### Chapter III - Cooperation

#### Articles 8-12 - Cooperation Group and CSIRT Network
- **Cooperation Group:** Strategic coordination among Member States and Commission
- **CSIRT Network:** Operational cooperation among CSIRTs
- **EU-CyCLONe:** European Cyber Crisis Liaison Organisation Network

### Chapter IV - Cybersecurity Risk Management and Reporting Obligations

#### Article 21 - Cybersecurity Risk Management Measures

**Core security requirements for all qualifying entities:**

| Paragraph | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| 2(a) | Risk analysis and information system security policies |
| 2(b) | Incident handling (prevention, detection, response) |
| 2(c) | Business continuity and crisis management |
| 2(d) | Supply chain security (including acquisition/development) |
| 2(e) | Security in network and information systems acquisition, development, maintenance |
| 2(f) | Vulnerability handling and disclosure |
| 2(g) | Assessment of effectiveness of risk management measures |
| 2(h) | Basic cyber hygiene and cybersecurity training |
| 2(i) | Use of cryptography and encryption |
| 2(j) | Human resources security, access control, asset management |
| 2(k) | Multi-factor authentication and secured communications |
| 2(l) | Security of communications and data transfer |
| 2(m) | Emergency plans, backup management, disaster recovery |
| 2(n) | Environmental control (physical, climatic, installation security) |

#### Article 22 - Supply Chain Security
Specific requirements for supply chain risk management:
- Security of products and services
- Vulnerabilities in supply chain
- Quality of cybersecurity products
- Coordination with suppliers

#### Article 23 - Reporting Obligations

**Significant Incident Definition (Paragraph 3):**

An incident shall be considered significant if:
1. It has caused or is capable of causing severe operational disruption
2. It has affected or is capable of affecting service provision
3. Any of the following thresholds:
   - Economic impact: Substantial material damage
   - Operational impact: Significant disruption of essential services
   - User impact: Large number of users affected
   - Data impact: Personal data breach affecting many individuals

**Reporting Timeline:**
| Deadline | Content |
|----------|---------|
| 24 hours | Early warning (if significant impact suspected) |
| 72 hours | Incident notification with initial assessment |
| 1 month | Final report with detailed analysis |

**Recipients:**
- Competent authority or CSIRT
- Potentially affected entities
- Cross-border coordination (if applicable)

### Chapter V - Essential and Important Entities

#### Article 24 - Essential Entities

**Categories (Annex I):**
- Energy (electricity, oil, gas, hydrogen, heating/cooling)
- Transport (air, rail, water, road)
- Banking and financial markets
- Health (healthcare, medical devices)
- Drinking and waste water
- Digital infrastructure (IXPs, DNS, TLD, cloud, data centers, CDNs)
- ICT service management (managed security services)
- Public administration (central government)
- Space

**Classification criteria:**
- Medium or large enterprises in Annex I sectors
- Public administration bodies
- Trust service providers (regardless of size)
- TLD registries (regardless of size)
- DNS service providers (regardless of size)

#### Article 25 - Important Entities

**Categories (Annex II):**
- Postal and courier services
- Waste management
- Chemicals manufacture/production/distribution
- Food production/processing/distribution
- Manufacturing (medical devices, computers, electronics, machinery, vehicles)
- Digital providers (online marketplaces, search engines, social networks)
- Research organizations

**Classification criteria:**
- Medium or large enterprises in Annex II sectors

#### Article 26 - Registration and Competent Authorities

**Lead Authority Determination:**
1. Entity operates in single Member State → That state's authority
2. Entity operates in multiple Member States:
   - Main establishment (place of central administration)
   - Where cybersecurity decisions are made
   - Where most employees are located
   - Where highest turnover is generated

**Registration requirements:**
- Register with competent authority of lead Member State
- Provide required information (name, address, sector, etc.)
- Update information when changes occur

### Chapter VI - Supervision and Enforcement

#### Articles 27-33 - Supervision and Ex-ante Supervision
Powers of competent authorities:
- Request information
- Conduct inspections
- Access premises
- Review documents
- Interview personnel

#### Article 34 - Administrative Sanctions

**Maximum Administrative Fines:**

| Entity Type | Maximum Amount | Alternative |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| Essential Entity | €10,000,000 | 2% of global annual turnover |
| Important Entity | €7,000,000 | 1.4% of global annual turnover |

**Proportionality factors:**
- Gravity and duration of infringement
- Damage caused
- Intentional or negligent character
- History of infringements
- Cooperation with authorities
- Other aggravating or mitigating factors

**Additional enforcement measures:**
- Binding instructions
- Implementation of recommendations
- Compliance audits
- Publication of sanctions

#### Articles 35-36 - Liability and Enforcement Cooperation
- Protection of reporting persons (whistleblowers)
- Cross-border enforcement cooperation

## Significant Incident Thresholds

### Qualitative Thresholds
An incident is significant if it:
1. Causes severe operational disruption
2. Affects service provision
3. Causes material damage to service recipients
4. Results in personal data breach requiring notification under GDPR

### Quantitative Thresholds (Guidance)
While specific thresholds vary by sector, indicative thresholds include:

| Impact Type | Threshold |
|-------------|-----------|
| Economic | €1,000,000+ financial loss |
| Operational | 12+ hours service unavailability |
| User Impact | 100,000+ users affected |
| Data | 100,000+ individuals' data affected |

**Note:** Member States and sector-specific regulations may define sector-specific thresholds.

## Crosswalks: NIS2 and Other Regulations

### NIS2 ↔ GDPR

| NIS2 | GDPR | Intersection |
|------|------|--------------|
| Article 21(2)(j) | Article 32 | Security of processing |
| Article 23 | Articles 33-34 | Personal data breach notification |
| Significant incident | Personal data breach | Coordinated notification |

**Key considerations:**
- Personal data breaches that are NIS2 significant incidents require both notifications
- Security measures overlap (encryption, access control)
- Competent authorities and DPAs should coordinate

### NIS2 ↔ DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act)

| NIS2 | DORA | Intersection |
|------|------|--------------|
| Article 21 | Article 6-16 | ICT risk management |
| Article 23 | Article 18-26 | ICT-related incident reporting |
| Article 22 | Article 28 | ICT third-party risk |
| - | Article 25 | Digital operational resilience testing |

**Key considerations:**
- Financial entities must comply with both (DORA is lex specialis for finance)
- Harmonized reporting thresholds
- Joint supervision by financial and NIS2 authorities

### NIS2 ↔ CER Directive (Critical Entities Resilience)

| NIS2 | CER Directive | Intersection |
|------|---------------|--------------|
| Annex I (Energy, Transport, etc.) | Annex I | Overlapping sectors |
| Cybersecurity | All-hazards resilience | Complementary coverage |
| Digital | Physical | Joint entities in scope |

**Key considerations:**
- CER covers all-hazards (physical, natural, cyber)
- NIS2 focuses specifically on cybersecurity
- Entities in both must address both sets of requirements

### NIS2 ↔ Cyber Resilience Act (CRA)

| NIS2 | CRA | Intersection |
|------|-----|--------------|
| Article 22 (supply chain) | Security requirements | Product security |
| Entity obligations | Manufacturer obligations | Complementary |
| - | Vulnerability reporting | Aligned timelines |

**Key considerations:**
- CRA applies to manufacturers of products with digital elements
- NIS2 applies to entities using such products
- Joint coverage of supply chain security

## National Transposition Variations

### Overview

Member States have flexibility in transposing NIS2, leading to potential variations:

1. **Broader scope:** Some states may include additional sectors
2. **Stricter requirements:** Some may impose additional security measures
3. **Larger entities:** Some may lower size thresholds
4. **Different authorities:** Designation of competent authorities

### Notable Variations

| Member State | Notable Variations |
|--------------|-------------------|
| **Romania** | **GEO 155/2024 + Law 124/2025** - DNSC (National Cyber Security Directorate) as competent authority. **Key feature:** DNSC may designate entities as essential/important on a case-by-case basis even where size criteria are not fulfilled, if disruption would have significant societal/economic consequences (e.g., sole provider of critical service). Registration via NIS2@RO platform. stricter executive liability. |
| Germany | Integration with existing BSI frameworks |
| France | ANSSI oversight expansion |
| Netherlands | NCSC coordination requirements |
| Spain | CN-PIC integration |
| Italy | ACN implementation |
| Poland | CSIRT GOV coordination |

#### Romania - Detailed Implementation

**Legal Framework:**
- **Primary:** Government Emergency Ordinance (GEO) No. 155/2024 (31 December 2024)
- **Amending:** Law No. 124/2025 (approved July 2025, entered into force 10 July 2025)
- **Implementing Orders:** Order 1/2025 (registration), Order 2/2025 (risk assessment methodology)

**Competent Authority:** 
- **DNSC** (National Cyber Security Directorate / Direcția Națională de Securitate Cibernetică)
- Website: [www.dnsc.ro](https://www.dnsc.ro)

**Key Romanian Specificities:**

1. **Designation Authority (Article 9):**
   DNSC may designate entities as essential or important **regardless of size thresholds** if:
   - They are the sole provider of a service essential for critical social/economic activities
   - They provide services dependent on national interest ICT infrastructure
   - Service disruption would have significant impact on public safety, national security, or public health

2. **Hospitals and Healthcare:**
   - Healthcare providers are listed as **Essential Entities** (Annex 1)
   - **Hospitals, clinics, and pharmaceutical distributors** are explicitly mentioned
   - Municipal hospitals (like Spitalul Municipal Mangalia) may be designated as essential based on:
     * Critical role in regional healthcare provision
     * Being the sole/primary healthcare provider for a municipality
     * Potential impact on public health from service disruption

3. **Registration Deadlines:**
   - Registration with DNSC via NIS2@RO platform (30 days from 20 August 2025)
   - Risk assessment within 60 days of DNSC confirmation
   - Maturity self-assessment within 60 days of risk assessment submission
   - Remediation plan within 30 days of maturity assessment

4. **Executive Liability:**
   - Personal liability of management for non-compliance
   - Mandatory cybersecurity training for management
   - Independent cybersecurity officer required

5. **Fines (in RON):**
   - Essential entities: Up to RON 500,000 (approx. €100,000) + EU NIS2 fines
   - Important entities: Up to RON 300,000 (approx. €60,000) + EU NIS2 fines
   - Repeat offenders: 50% increase over applicable fine cap

**Practical Note:**
Entities in the "gap" between medium and large enterprise criteria (e.g., 250-500 employees with turnover €10M-€50M) should still register with DNSC if they provide critical services. The DNSC will assess on a case-by-case basis whether designation as essential/important is warranted based on the criticality of services provided.

**Sources:**
- GEO 155/2024 (Official Gazette No. 1279/31.12.2024)
- Law 124/2025 (Official Gazette No. 711/10.07.2025)
- DNSC Order 1/2025 and Order 2/2025 (Official Gazette No. 820/20.08.2025)
